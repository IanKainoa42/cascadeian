  • Goal. Default objective is WRECK: wreck every opposing alliance. CTF, Raid, Zomboids and Red Rover are on the Objective step (Pro).
  • Command. Battles run in 60-second waves. When a wave ends the sim auto-pauses into a 15-second Regroup (the outer ring cascades away during it — the crumble is the countdown) — a small countdown strip floats over the top of the battlefield (visible in fullscreen too) with a Ready button, but it doesn't block the board: the whole map stays clickable so you can keep planning routes and coverage right through the timeout. Hit Ready (or wait out the clock) to launch the next wave. You can still Pause/Run freely mid-wave.
  • Deploy. Every squad starts in its own glowing corner hex. Select a trooper, then tap or drag to any open small hex inside that boundary. You cannot deploy into the rest of the arena. In hotseat or multiplayer, each human commander readies their squad; battle starts automatically when everyone is ready.
  • Start battle. Step 1 sets match format, objective, real-time vs turn-based, and turn style. Step 2 picks squad and aiming. Step 3 builds the battlefield. DEPLOY opens formation setup; START BATTLE launches wave 1 after every required commander is ready.
  • Vision. No god's-eye toggle -- fog applies as long as your side is still in the fight. Every teammate contributes its local radius plus wherever its weapon actually has line of sight; the trooper you have selected sees a little further and clearer than the rest. Setting your focus point or planning a route doesn't reveal anything by itself -- only real sightlines do. A gunshot briefly reveals the shooter's hex even through fog, since a shot is something you'd actually notice. Once your whole squad is wiped, or if it's AI-vs-AI with no human seat at all, fog lifts entirely — nothing left to hide from.
  • Camera. The default view frames your squad with a couple of hexes of context instead of showing the whole arena. Use the battle menu to zoom out to the full map when you need the larger read.
  • Board colors. Red hexes are enemy fire coverage or dangerous move destinations. Dark covered hexes are fog — and fog only lifts along real sightlines: nothing is ever seen through a wall (a fired gun briefly pings its muzzle hex, that's the one exception). Pulsing green hexes are safe reachable moves — a white line with arrows shows the order once you set one. Black/white marked hard cover is anchored: it blocks sight, cannot be shoved, and never breaks.
  • Troopers. RF = Rifle, SN = Sniper, SG = Shotgun, SH = Shield, BR = Burst. Each role also has a different silhouette.
  • Squad. Pick a loadout on the Squad step: Duo (2), Trio (3: Shotgun, Rifle, Burst -- a small elite fireteam, Rainbow Six-style), Quad (4), Fireteam (5), Breach (5), or Custom (6, Pro).
  • Facing turns, it doesn't snap. A unit's cone rotates toward wherever it's told to look at a limited rate (about a second for a full turn) instead of jumping instantly. Its real, currently-aimed cone (which is what combat and vision actually use) can lag behind its intent for a moment -- that's your window to catch someone mid-turn. Troopers defend themselves, but with real attention: a visible enemy within ~3 hexes must first be noticed -- near-instant if it appears where they're already looking, up to ~a second if it pops up behind them, slower while marching and slower still sprinting -- then the body swings around, takes a beat to steady the aim, and opens up. Once noticed, eyes track the enemy until line of sight breaks. Getting shot skips the noticing: the hit itself says where the shooter is.
  • Auto-defense has a flinch. Getting hit costs a brief beat (about a third of a second) before a unit can fire back, on top of its normal reload. A surprise shot buys you a real head start.
  • Spray weapons. Shotgun and Burst (Tommy gun) fire 3 rounds per trigger pull instead of one clean shot. Each round independently picks a target among whoever's in cone right now, weighted by how easy a shot it is -- one enemy in the open eats all 3, several enemies means the rounds spread across them. Burst is an actual rapid burst — its three rounds leave ~0.1s apart (brrt), reloads quickly, and its spray carries deep into its long range; Shotgun is wide and close (a genuine buckshot spray, all pellets at once).
  • Facing. Each unit snaps to one of 12 aim positions around the hex, covering sides and corners instead of only six lanes.
  • The arena shrinks. In the last 15 seconds of every wave the doomed outer ring glows amber. Then, during the 15-second regroup, it cascades away hex by hex around the circle — the crumbling ring IS the timer: when the cascade completes, the next wave begins. A trooper caught on a crumbling hex is hurled across the arena to the far side (orders dropped, awareness reset — landing disoriented is the price of dawdling); CTF home markers and a loose flag slide in with it. Sight and shots still cross the chasm — only feet can't. A green sandbag charge drops at the start of every wave and keeps landing mid-wave (~every 20s): grab it for 10 seconds of rapid fire (halved reloads). It hides in the fog — scouting finds it — and the AIs hunt it, so digging in has a price. Regroup timeouts also tighten as the arena shrinks (15s down to 6s). Ordinary barriers are much tougher now: every stray round that strikes one cracks it, and the fifteenth hit crumbles it. Anchored black/white hard cover never crumbles. The field tightens every wave down to a small final arena, so claim ground that will still exist.
  • Coverage. Each weapon has its own cone: snipers watch a narrow long slice, rifles hold a medium lane, and shotgun/shield units cover wider short arcs. A marching unit's cone narrows a little — still open, but holding position aims better.
  • Line of sight is a real, unobstructed line. A trooper can't see, notice, or shoot an enemy unless there's a clear direct line between them — a wall (or another body) sitting on that line blocks it entirely, no peeking around the corner and no shooting through it. Once you do have a line, cover grazing the target's body still trims the odds: if a wall edge hides most of them, the shot is capped near that fraction no matter how good the angle or range otherwise looks. Being in range is not enough — you need the line.
  • Barriers are furniture. Tap an ordinary wall adjacent to your selected trooper to shoulder it one hex forward and take its place (costs about 1.5 march steps). Build your own cover line, or push a wall into a firing lane. Anchored hard cover is fixed in place.
  • Cover is directional. A wall only counts as cover if it's actually on the side facing the shooter — tucking in behind a bunker works, but it won't save you from a flank or a shot from your unprotected side. Stand out in the open with no cover and no allies nearby and you're now hit almost every trigger pull someone gets a bead on you — don't linger in open ground.
  • Peek around the corner. Firing with a wall of your own tucked against the hex you're standing in, angled toward whoever you're shooting at, braces the shot for up to +20% accuracy — the same corner that's covering you from their return fire also steadies your aim. Holding any wall is a fortified posture: +2 vision (you see further from cover), quicker reactions (you notice and fire back faster), and the wall shields the covered side. The flip side — a trooper caught in the open shoots worse (up to −28% accuracy, nothing to brace against), so fighting from cover beats trading in the open.
  • Aiming is mostly automatic. A marching trooper looks straight down its heading, out to its weapon's range; a trooper holding position watches the open approaches instead of the ground -- it ranks the lanes an enemy could come down and sweeps its cone across the most dangerous ones (a sniper commits to one, a rifle covers two, a shotgun fans three). Any trooper swings its cone onto an enemy the instant it notices one and opens fire — you don't have to aim to defend yourself. Tap outside the green move surround to override and point a specific trooper's line of sight where you want (watch a flank, pre-aim a doorway); it holds that watch until it spots a threat. The line-of-sight focus draws as a fine amber dotted line ending in an eye mark. Only the selected unit's coverage renders; angled peeks can light partial triangle wedges instead of a whole hex.
  • Movement. Real-time uses the pulsing green walking range: tap a green hex to move or drag through it to trace a route. Teleport is its own mode: the same range becomes warp range and movement instantly tunnels to the destination instead of marching. Every warp has a magenta recharge ring, and each extra hex makes that cooldown longer. Turn-based keeps its move-and-action economy.
  • The burst. Immediate moves are a full-tilt sprint — the trooper covers the green step fast and is 45% harder to hit, but can't fire during the dash. There's no separate sprint control; short repositioning is intentionally quick, while longer advances come from chaining steps under fire.
  • Cohesion — a sweet spot, not "more is always better." Two adjacent allies is the peak bonus; stack up 4+ deep and the bonus tapers off and then goes negative — you're in each other's way and an easy cluster to hit, on top of literally not being able to fire through a teammate. A nearby Shield unit gives everyone around it extra protection beyond a normal trooper, offsetting its own short range. Ally links render as a faint dotted mesh behind your move/focus lines so they don't compete for attention.
  • Flanking. A shot arriving from outside a unit's own facing arc (more than 90° off where it's watching) catches it off guard: +50% hit chance. A flanking hit's tracer flashes magenta and is tagged FLANK instead of the usual pale gold.
  • Self-defense. Units fire automatically when enemies enter their coverage cone. If caught in danger without a shot, they may evade to safer cover.
  • Cooldown rings. The gold ring sweeping clockwise is move cooldown; the orange ring sweeping the other way is fire cooldown. Both can show at once.
  • Tracers. Every shot sends a bright laser segment floating from shooter to target — the farther the shot, the longer it takes to arrive, so a sniper's bolt gives you real time to see it coming.
  • Bullets travel for real. A shot's hit/miss doesn't land the instant it's fired — damage only applies once the tracer actually reaches the target hex. Sprint or step off that hex before it arrives and the shot whiffs, no matter how good the odds looked at the moment of firing.
  • Damage. Hits chip health; a unit is removed at 0 HP. Snipers and close shotguns/bursts hit harder; rifles hit hard (2 damage) out to 3 hexes — the steady heavy hitter; shields survive longer but only carry a weak sidearm. Snipers reload slower than the other roles and fumble badly up close (35-60% accuracy inside 2 hexes) -- their range and accuracy come at a real close-quarters cost.
  • Map fairness. Every squad is guaranteed at least a small bunker in front of its spawn. Distinct wall clusters stay separated instead of fusing into one mega-wall, some black/white hard-cover blocks anchor the center and bunkers, and a couple of narrow winding corridors get carved into each map to crawl through.
  • Hit math. Adjacent shots are near certain. Snipers keep accuracy at range with narrow coverage, rifles are balanced, shotguns are broader and close, and shields have a weak wide sidearm cone.
  • Controls. Tap a trooper to select it (or use the bottom roster dock — tap a chip, or ⟩⟩ to cycle). Tap or drag inside the pulsing green range to move; in Teleport mode that command becomes an instant warp and the range disappears until the distance-scaled recharge completes. Tap outside the range to set its eyes down that lane. Tap an adjacent ordinary wall to shoulder it forward. The selected card's ⊙ WATCH button pins its line of sight. On a phone in landscape the trooper cards dock to the right edge so the board keeps the full height. The same gestures work on a finger, an Apple Pencil, and a Mac trackpad or mouse.
  • Turn-based mode. Set Mode: Turn-based in setup for a chess-like battle: teams alternate, and each trooper gets a simple sequence: move, then set line of sight. Move up to its own range (sniper 2 · rifle/shield 3 · shotgun/burst 4); after the move, tap an enemy to shoot or tap open board to set that trooper's sight lane and overwatch, then the dock advances to the next ready trooper. You can also tap SET SIGHT to hold position and aim. The first enemy crossing a set lane eats a reaction shot. A stunned trooper loses exactly one character turn, cannot move, fire, or set sight during it, and then recovers. Green hexes are safe moves; red-tinted hexes are reachable but watched. Whatever nobody seals is the window you maneuver through. END passes the turn; win by wipe (or CTF). No wave clock, no arena shrink — pure positioning. And the fog is real: you only see what your squad's cones and close-range sight cover — and a wall blocks both, no sensing through solid cover (shared — a scout spots for the sniper). An un-spotted enemy is invisible; lose sight of one and a dashed ? marks its last known spot — scout that hex to confirm or clear it. Enemy overwatch is a hidden trap until you can actually see the watcher, and reaction fire from an unseen watcher still hits — that's an ambush. The AI plays by the same rules: it only hunts what it has spotted, so slipping past its cones is a real play.
  • AI skill. On the Squad step: Recruit, Regular or Veteran. It does not touch the dice -- every trooper's hit chance is identical at all three. What changes is how well the enemy is run: how often it re-tasks its squad, how many troopers it can redirect at once, how long it must hold you in view before it may fire, and how quickly it reacts once it has. Veteran squads flow; Recruit squads are slow to notice you and slower to answer. Real-time only -- turn-based runs a different AI.
  • Service record. Your wins, losses, kills and best Zomboids wave are kept on this device and shown under the wordmark on the home screen. Zomboids never counts as a loss -- it is endless, so it logs your best wave instead. Nothing is uploaded anywhere; Reset record at the bottom of this guide wipes it.
  • Blast knockback. Optional and experimental: when a unit drops to 0 HP, the impact shoves adjacent enemies one hex away if there is room.
  • Multiplayer. Host a room to get a 4-letter code, share it, and whoever joins takes the next open team (up to as many teams as the Format allows; the rest stay AI). The host is the only device actually running the battle -- everyone else just sends move/focus/ready requests and watches the result. The connection now watches itself: if a phone lock or app switch kills the link (it dies silently — nobody gets a disconnect event), both sides notice within seconds — the joiner auto-rejoins the same room (their seat is held and handed back), and until they return their team plays as AI. A room chat is built in. Turn-based works online too: the host picks Mode: Turn-based (in setup, or mid-room via ⋯ → Battle setup) — each player takes their turn from their own device, with their own fog, and the host resolves everything.
  • Event feed. The scrolling panel above the field logs recent shots, hits, and captures, not just the single latest line in the status bar.
